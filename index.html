CS 210 Project: Analyzing Tweeting Patterns

Welcome to my data science project! Below are visualizations representing various aspects of the data analysis. The visualizations are all interactive, so they may take some time to load.

Description of the Dataset

I obtained this data by requesting it from my Twitter archive. It ranges from mid-2019 to November 2023. There is a period that seems to be ‘missing‘ in the data, but it is organically missing. This means that it was not present in the archive to begin with. The reason for this is that I had deleted a lot of my twetes of that periodon my own a couple of years ago.

The dataset consisted of around 12800 tweets and retweets. The raw data can not be disclosed for privacy concerns, but each tweet had an associated number of likes, number of retweets, classified language, hashtags and mentions. Another set of data I used was that concerned with advertisements which consisted of around 24000 instances of advertisements over the period of me having Twitter, approximately 7 years.

This data can be found in the GitHub repository for this website under /data.

Make-Up of My Tweets

This visualization shows how many of my tweets are original tweets, replies, private quotes, public quotes, or retweets.

This becomes interesting information after I explain why I would choose to do a circle quote versus a public quote. If I make a circle quote, visible only to people in my circle, which used to be an old Twitter feature, I would often be very critical, to put it nicely, of what I'm quoting. In other words, this shows the fraction representing the number of times I expressed my aggressive criticism in comparison to all of my expressions.

A more in depth analysis here would require that this frequency is mapped over time to see how my online attitudes are changing over time if they are changing. The tweets/retweets attitude is already mapped below, and adding the quotes and replies would definitely add a lot more to the analysis.


Mode Hour

Over time, the hour during which I tweeted the most changed. However, there's still a peak that remained pretty much the same: evening.

This is a very detailed graph that shows the hour during which I tweeted most for every day in the dataset. Often, the peaks would be around the same time for consecutive days, given that I am a person who has a routine. Because of my routine, it would make sense that most tweets are either early morning or around evening.

We can see some patterns that map to events in my life. During my A Level exams, there were many fluctuations in my routine, which can be seen in the visible May-June periods in 2021 and 2022. During summers, we'll have more points during the early hours of the morning in comparison to winter. In winter 2022, I would never be up before 06:00, so all of the points are away from that time.


Likes and Retweets per Day

This chart shows the distribution of likes and retweets per day.

The number of tweets is somewhat constant with some sudden peaks every once in a while. These peaks are always due to Tweets that became popular for any reason. Mostly, these tweets were jokes or silly screenshots.


Number of Tweets versus Number of Retweets

It's interesting to see how this pattern changes during big events. As an Egyptian, it was expected that I react during the Egyptian MeToo movement and again during the devaluation of the currency in October of 2022. The Israeli aggression on Gaza also led to a rise in the number of retweets in comparison to tweets, and this might be because I'm not a professional on the matter, so I retweet rather than say something new. A number of tweets generally decreased during that period too, which makes sense as well. I find it shameful to tweet like I usually would when people are being massacred, so I didn't tweet as much.

There is also a peak around the Summer of 2023 which is a time I strongly reconnected with friends back home through online activity.

One would think that this graph and the previous graph should align, but they don't. This might be because of the audience difference. When I first started actively tweeting, I did not have much of a following, unlike now. This can explain the general rise in likes and thus explain why these graphs are not as complimentary as they should be intuitively.

Takeaways!

Mostly, my graphs confirm what I know about myself. I'm usually active towards the evening, and sometimes ea I don't tweet as much during a human rights disaster but rather retweet. I tweet a lot when a crisis happens in Egypt, my home. The data confirms all of this. I also had some personal issues in the Summer of 2023, and I found solace in my friends, which explains the general rise in tweet rate that can be observed during that period. Sentiment analysis of the tweets would have confirmed that. Preliminary analysis showed very negative sentiments during this period in comparison to others.

I plan on adding the result of the natural language analysis as I am sure that this will show a lot more than the quantitative analysis. However, given the multilingual nature of the dataset, this is proving difficult as the applicable approaches will kill the uniqueness of my dataset. The current approach is to transliterate the Romanized Arabic to proper Arabic and use Arabic NLP models to analyze it.

Generally, the data obtained so far shows a lot about my patterns and routines. My routine can be traced from my activity on Twitter, which is where I'm most active out of all social media platforms. The next step would be analyzing the tweets during peak activity and establishing a scheme that is able to predict whether or not I will be active and responsve on Twitter given some happenings.
